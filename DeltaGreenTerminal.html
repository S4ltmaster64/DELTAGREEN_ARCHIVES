THE BROKEN CHAIN PROTOCOL

THE BROKEN CHAIN PROTOCOL is an assessment procedure administered to DELTAGREEN personnel suspected of anomalous exposure.

The protocol consists of a standardized verbal trigger and the subsequent analysis of the subject’s response. When presented with the trigger phrase, affected individuals have been observed to produce symbolic or poetic responses that correlate with active anomalous conditions.

TRIGGER PHRASE:
“DOES THE BLACK MOON HOWL?”

ADMINISTRATION:
Present the trigger phrase to the subject.
Record the response verbatim through a written medium.
Do not interrupt the subject.
Do not request clarification from the subject.
Review the BROKEN CHAIN PROTOCOL and identify the anomaly.

MASTER/STRANGER PROTOCOL

THE MASTER/STRANGER PROTOCOL is a structured interrogative framework used to evaluate DELTA GREEN personnel for cognitive degradation, anomalous influence, and compromised perception following suspected exposure to paranormal phenomena.

For the purposes of this protocol, anomalous cognitive influence is divided into two primary classifications:


MASTER-CLASS EFFECTS:

MASTER-CLASS EFFECTS are defined as anomalous influences that alter volitional structure, motivational hierarchy, or behavioral prioritization within an otherwise stable cognitive framework.

Subjects affected by MASTER-CLASS phenomena typically retain: 
Continuous autobiographical identity.
Coherent sensory perception.
Stable linguistic and memory function.

However, decision-making processes may exhibit:
Reprioritization of mission objectives.
Unexplained protective or adversarial bias toward specific entities, locations, or concepts.
Persistence of behavioral patterns inconsistent with baseline psychological profile.

MASTER-CLASS influence is therefore considered a distortion of agency rather than perception.

Subjects may not recognize impairment.


STRANGER-CLASS EFFECTS:

STRANGER_CLASS EFFECTS are defined as anomalous influences that disrupt the stability of perceptual anchoring, referential mapping, or identity continuity within the subject’s cognitive model of reality.

Subjects affected by STRANGER-CLASS phenomena may exhibit:
Failure to consistently recognize individuals, locations, or objects.
Discrepancies in environmental recall across short time intervals.
Conflicting or unstable descriptions of identical stimuli.
Perceptual substitution or misidentification of familiar references.

STRANGER-CLASS influence is therefore considered a distortion of reference rather than intent.

Subjects may not recognize impairment.

ADDENDUM A: MASTER AND STRANGER RELATIONSHIPS

MASTER and STRANGER classifications are not mutually exclusive. 

In combined exposure cases, subjects may present:
Stable perception with compromised agency (MASTER-DOMINANT)
Stable agency with compromised perception (STRANGER-DOMINANT)
Reclusive instability wherein both perception and agency are mutually reinforcing erroneous interpretations of reality.

Such cases are considered high-risk due to the reduced reliability of self-reporting.
